# Minimum Engagement Delivery Framework
## Executive Narrative

**Author:** Amar Kutwal | **Date:** May 2026 | **For:** Nick (TAM Manager)

## The Challenge

The TAM role is changing. With the EOP → Enterprise transition driving unprecedented customer volumes, we're managing 8-12 customers where we once managed 4-5. The question isn't whether we can maintain the same engagement depth — we can't. The question is: **how do we deliver consistent, measurable value at double the portfolio size without quality degradation?**

This framework is the answer.

---

## Why This Framework Exists

Three forces converged:

1. **Volume:** EOP transitions are adding 4-6 new Enterprise Support customers to each TAM portfolio in 2026. My portfolio will reach 6-8 customers by Q3, potentially 10 by Q4.

2. **Heterogeneity:** Not all customers are equal. Allied Publicity Services runs 49 accounts at $120K/month — they need deep, frequent engagement. BlockTech has 9 accounts at $30K/month — they need efficient, targeted engagement. A one-size-fits-all model fails both.

3. **Complexity:** Partner-resold customers like Vopak (via InQdo) operate under different commercial rules. Without a documented model, we risk compliance failures — particularly around cost data that belongs to the partner relationship.

The framework addresses all three by introducing **tiered engagement**, **efficiency mechanisms**, and **structured guardrails**.

---

## How It Works

### Tiered Engagement

Every customer is classified into Small, Medium, or Large based on account count, monthly spend, and architectural complexity. The tier determines:

- **How much time** they receive (5-15% of TAM week)
- **How deep** their SSP is (3-4 milestones for Small vs. 8-10 for Large)
- **How often** they get face time (monthly or bi-weekly cadence calls)
- **What format** works best (async updates for Small, dedicated sessions for Large)

This isn't about giving less to smaller customers — it's about giving the *right amount* effectively.

### Automation & Efficiency

Seven SOPs (Project Ulduz) automate the repetitive work that previously consumed 5-7 hours per week: morning prep, case triage, cadence call preparation, customer onboarding, and SSP creation. That time is reinvested in strategic customer work.

Additionally, one-to-many workshops, async updates, and self-service enablement (Trusted Advisor, DevOps Agent, Q Developer) reduce the reactive burden on TAM time.

### The SSP Pipeline

The framework isn't just an engagement model — it's an SSP generation engine. Phase 2 of the rollout systematically collects the data (health events, architecture gaps, case patterns, customer priorities) that the Support Plan Builder SOP transforms into draft milestones. Without Phase 2 running consistently, each SSP requires 4-6 hours of manual research. With it, creation drops to 2-3 hours because the intelligence is already collected through daily operations.

## Measuring Success

The framework includes explicit success metrics across three dimensions:

1. **Framework effectiveness:** Are we managing more customers without quality drops? (Cadence adherence, SSP completion rate, SBR delivery rate)

2. **Customer outcomes:** Are customers healthier? (CHI scores, case volume trends, milestone completion, renewal rates)

3. **Efficiency:** Are the mechanisms working? (Hours saved, async adoption, self-service uptake)

Leading indicators flag problems early — if cadences are being skipped, CHI drops below 40, or milestones stall for 6+ weeks, immediate action is triggered before customers notice.
